Beginner's Guide to Caring for Chicks

A comprehensive guide to raising healthy, happy chicks

Caring for baby chicks can be a rewarding experience, whether you're raising them for eggs, meat, or as pets. This guide will walk you through the essentials of chick care to help your fluffy little ones grow into healthy chickens.

Healthy Treats for Chicks

After your chicks are one week old, you can begin offering treats in moderation (no more than 10% of their diet). Treats should be introduced gradually and should never replace their chick starter feed, which contains the essential nutrients they need for proper growth.

DIY Treat Options

  • Scrambled eggs: Excellent source of protein, can be fed from week 1
  • Chopped greens: Lettuce, kale, spinach (avoid iceberg lettuce), introduce after week 2
  • Vegetable scraps: Cucumber, zucchini, carrots (finely chopped), after week 2
  • Fruits (limited): Berries, melon, apple pieces (remove seeds), introduce after week 3, limit due to sugar content
  • Herbs: Fresh oregano, thyme, parsley, mint - can help boost immune system, after week 2

Important: Always introduce new treats gradually and monitor chicks for any digestive issues. Remove uneaten fresh treats after a few hours to prevent spoilage and maintain brooder cleanliness.

Getting Started: What You'll Need

Brooder Setup

  • Brooder container: A plastic tote, wooden box, or commercial brooder that provides at least 0.5 sq ft per chick initially (increasing as they grow)
  • Heat source: A heat lamp or brooder plate to maintain proper temperature
  • Thermometer: To monitor brooder temperature
  • Bedding: Pine shavings (avoid cedar, which can be toxic) or paper towels for the first few days
  • Feeder: A chick feeder that reduces waste and prevents contamination
  • Waterer: A chick waterer that prevents drowning and keeps water clean
  • Chick starter feed: 18-20% protein specifically formulated for baby chicks
  • Chick grit: Helps chicks digest food properly (not needed if using commercial chick starter)

Key Factors When Choosing a Location

  • Temperature control: Can you maintain 90-95°F initially, then reduce by 5°F weekly?
  • Predator protection: Is the area secure from all potential predators (including pets)?
  • Monitoring ease: Can you check chicks multiple times daily without hassle?
  • Ventilation: Is there good airflow without drafts directly on chicks?
  • Electricity: Reliable power source for heating equipment?
  • Noise tolerance: Chicks get progressively louder as they grow
  • Dust management: Chick down and bedding create significant dust
  • Space requirements: Need for expanding room as chicks grow
Pro tip: For most beginners, a garage or spare room/bathroom is ideal for the first few weeks, then transition to a more permanent outdoor solution once chicks are partially feathered (around 4 weeks). Wherever you choose, ensure the temperature is stable and you can easily access the chicks for frequent care and monitoring.

Temperature Management

Temperature is crucial for chick survival. Follow this temperature guide by age:

  • 1-7 days: 90-95°F (32-35°C)
  • Week 2: 85-90°F (29-32°C)
  • Week 3: 80-85°F (27-29°C)
  • Week 4: 75-80°F (24-27°C)
  • Week 5: 70-75°F (21-24°C)
  • Week 6+: 65-70°F (18-21°C)
Pro tip: Watch your chicks' behavior to gauge temperature:
  • Chicks huddled directly under heat source = too cold
  • Chicks spread out along edges of brooder = too hot
  • Chicks moving freely throughout brooder = just right

Lighting Requirements

Proper lighting is essential for healthy chick development and is separate from heat requirements. Heat and light serve different purposes in chick development.

Lighting Schedule

  • Day period: Provide 12-14 hours of light during the day
  • Night period: Ensure 10-12 hours of darkness for proper rest and development
  • Consistency: Keep lighting hours consistent each day (consider using a timer)
  • Transitions: Ideally, provide brief dawn/dusk transition periods rather than abrupt light changes

Light Intensity and Type

  • Daytime intensity: Moderate room lighting is sufficient - approximately the brightness of a normally lit room
  • Overnight: Complete darkness at night is beneficial for proper rest
  • Light color: Natural white light during the day is best for chick development
  • Avoid: Excessively bright lighting which can cause stress and may contribute to pecking behavior

Heating vs. Lighting

Heat Source Type Light Considerations Additional Notes
Brooder Plate Doesn't provide light, only heat Requires separate ambient room lighting during day; turn off lights at night for darkness
Standard Heat Lamp Provides both heat and bright light Can disrupt sleep if used 24/7; may need to be turned off at night if room is warm enough
Red Heat Lamp Provides heat with less disruptive red light Better for nighttime use if heat is needed 24/7, as red light is less disruptive to sleep
Pro tip: Studies have shown that continuous 24-hour lighting can negatively affect chick development, potentially causing stress, altered growth rates, and even behavioral issues. A regular day/night cycle promotes healthier development and better sleep patterns.

Feeding and Watering

Feed

  • Start with chick starter feed (18-20% protein) for the first 8 weeks
  • Feed should be available at all times (free choice)
  • After 8 weeks, transition to grower feed (16-18% protein)
  • Avoid treats for the first week; afterward, offer in moderation

Water

  • Provide clean, fresh water at all times
  • Keep waterers clean by rinsing daily and disinfecting weekly
  • Use room temperature water
  • Place waterers on a small platform once chicks are a few days old to prevent bedding from getting into the water

Health and Safety

Signs of a Healthy Chick

  • Active and alert
  • Eating and drinking regularly
  • Clean vent area
  • Clear eyes
  • Growing feathers evenly

Common Health Issues

  • Pasty butt: Dried droppings blocking the vent; clean gently with warm, damp cloth
  • Spraddle leg: Legs splaying outward; correct early with a bandage "hobble"
  • Respiratory issues: Sneezing, wheezing, nasal discharge; may require veterinary care
  • Coccidiosis: Bloody droppings; requires medication

Preventing Health Problems

  • Keep brooder clean and dry
  • Provide proper nutrition
  • Maintain appropriate temperature
  • Reduce stress by minimizing handling for the first few days
  • Use electrolytes in water during stressful periods

Handling Chicks

  • Wait until chicks are 1-2 days old before handling
  • Wash hands before and after handling chicks
  • Hold gently by placing one hand under the chick and cupping the other over its back
  • Keep handling sessions short (5-10 minutes) for young chicks
  • Gradually increase handling time to socialize chicks

Weeks 6-8: Preparing for the Outdoor Coop

Around 6-8 weeks of age, chicks will be ready to transition to an outdoor coop if weather permits. Prepare by:

  • Ensuring the coop is predator-proof
  • Installing proper ventilation while avoiding drafts
  • Providing appropriate roosting bars and nesting boxes
  • Introducing chicks to the coop during the day, returning them to the brooder at night initially
  • Gradually extending coop time until they stay overnight

Advanced Tips and Tricks

Brooder Setup

  • Vertical space management: Secure hardware cloth over open-top brooders to prevent escapes as chicks learn to jump and flutter.
  • Brooder box insulation: Line wooden brooder boxes with aluminum foil before adding bedding to create a moisture barrier and make cleaning easier.
  • Nighttime visibility: Use a red or blue nightlight instead of white light if chicks need overnight illumination - it's less disruptive to their sleep.
  • Removable liners: Place vinyl shelf liner or puppy pads under pine shavings for easy cleanup during bedding changes.
  • Brooder placement: Position the brooder in a quiet, draft-free area that maintains a stable temperature but still allows you to observe the chicks.

Feeding Innovations

  • Prevent drowning: Place clean marbles in shallow water dishes if using them instead of commercial waterers to prevent drowning.
  • DIY chick feeder: Use a clean plastic container with small holes cut near the bottom edge as an emergency feeder.
  • Reduce waste: Place a small piece of hardware cloth over open feeders to prevent chicks from scratching feed out.
  • Supplement boost: Add a tablespoon of plain yogurt to feed once weekly after week 2 to support digestive health.
  • Greens holder: Attach a small bundle of greens to the side of the brooder with a clip to keep greens clean and encourage natural pecking.

Health Management

  • DIY electrolyte solution: Mix 1 tsp sugar, 1/8 tsp salt, and 1/8 tsp baking soda in 1 cup of water for emergency electrolytes.
  • Vitamin boost: Add a few drops of apple cider vinegar to water (1 tsp per gallon) twice weekly to support immunity.
  • Gentle cleaning for pasty butt: Use a cotton ball soaked in warm water to gently soften dried droppings before removing.
  • Spraddle leg prevention: Use shelf liner or rubber drawer liner as brooder floor covering for the first few days to provide secure footing.
  • Temperature fluctuation buffer: Place a large rock or brick under the heat source that will absorb and slowly release heat if there's a power outage.

Socialization and Enrichment

  • Chick playground: Create small ramps or platforms in the brooder once chicks are active to encourage exercise and exploration.
  • Dust bath dish: After week 3, provide a small container with a mixture of fine sand and food-grade diatomaceous earth for dust bathing.
  • Background noise: Playing quiet radio music helps chicks get used to various sounds and reduces stress from sudden noises.
  • Mirror entertainment: Securely attach a small mirror to the side of the brooder for chicks to interact with and recognize themselves.
  • Training treats: Use small bits of mealworm to teach chicks to come when called - very useful for when they're older.

Time-Saving Techniques

  • Batch waterer cleaning: Keep multiple waterer bases ready so you can quickly swap them out for cleaning.
  • Feed storage: Store a week's worth of feed in easily accessible containers near the brooder to save time on daily feedings.
  • Multi-level brooder: As chicks grow, create a two-level brooder with a ramp to increase space without needing a larger footprint.
  • Scheduled observation: Set specific times each day for observing chicks to ensure you notice any health or behavioral changes.
  • Double brooder setup: For larger flocks, maintain two smaller brooders rather than one large one for easier cleaning and maintenance.

Money-Saving Ideas

  • Repurposed containers: Clean gallon milk jugs can be cut and used as temporary feeders or waterers in a pinch.
  • DIY chick toys: Paper towel tubes stuffed with treats encourage natural foraging behavior.
  • Brooder furniture: Use upside-down flowerpots as roosts and hiding spots in the brooder.
  • Heat conservation: Wrap reflective insulation around the outside of the brooder to improve heat efficiency.
  • Bedding extender: Top-dress soiled bedding with a thin layer of fresh bedding between full changes to reduce waste.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

  • Picking prevention: If chicks begin pecking at each other, dim the lighting slightly and ensure adequate space.
  • Cold chick recovery: If a chick is found cold and listless, warm it against your skin under your shirt before returning to brooder.
  • Weak leg remedy: Add a vitamin supplement containing vitamin B to water for a few days if chicks show leg weakness.
  • Pasting prevention: Reduce protein slightly if multiple chicks develop pasty butt; can indicate protein is too high.
  • Stressed chick calming: Place a few herbs like dried oregano or thyme in the brooder to create a calming effect.

Chicken Terminology for Beginners

Understanding the vocabulary used in chicken keeping will help you communicate with other chicken keepers and better understand care instructions.

Term Definition
Brooder The heated enclosure where baby chicks are raised until they're old enough to regulate their body temperature (usually 6-8 weeks).
Brooder plate A heating device that mimics a mother hen's warmth, providing heat from above rather than a heat lamp.
Chick A baby chicken of either sex, typically refers to birds under 8 weeks old.
Coccidiosis A common parasitic disease in chickens caused by protozoa that affects the intestinal tract, often prevented with medicated starter feed.
Cockerel A young male chicken less than one year old.
Comb The fleshy red crest on top of a chicken's head.
Crop Part of a chicken's digestive system; an expandable pouch in the throat that stores food before digestion.
Feather pecking A behavior where chickens peck at each other's feathers, which can lead to injury if not addressed.
Gizzard A muscular part of a chicken's digestive system that grinds food, works with grit to break down food particles.
Grit Small, insoluble stones or commercial product that chickens ingest to help grind food in their gizzard.
Keel The extension of the breastbone that runs down the middle of the chicken's chest.
Medicated feed Starter feed containing medication (usually amprolium) to prevent coccidiosis in chicks.
Molt The process of shedding and regrowing feathers, which typically occurs annually.
Pasty butt A condition where droppings stick to and block a chick's vent, which can be fatal if not addressed.
Pullet A young female chicken less than one year old.
Spraddle leg A condition where a chick's legs splay outward, making it difficult or impossible to walk normally.
Vent The external opening of a chicken's cloaca, which serves as the single exit point for the digestive, reproductive, and urinary tracts.
Wattles The fleshy red lobes that hang beneath a chicken's beak.
Pro tip: Learning these terms will not only help you understand chicken care instructions better but will also help you communicate effectively with veterinarians and experienced chicken keepers if you need assistance.
